Step 1: Introduction

The dataset records major power outages in the continental United States from January 2000 to July 2016. It contains 1,534 rows (one per outage event) and 56 columns capturing when and where each outage occurred, its cause and duration, the number of customers affected, regional climate characteristics, electricity consumption patterns, and state-level economic and demographic indicators.

Central question: What are the biggest factors that determine how long a power outage lasts?

Understanding outage duration matters because longer outages pose greater risks to public safety, disrupt hospitals and emergency services, and impose significant economic costs. By identifying the features most predictive of duration, utilities and policymakers could allocate repair crews and emergency resources more effectively.

Step 2: Data Cleaning and Exploratory Data Analysis

Data Cleaning Steps

The raw data is an Excel file with several metadata rows at the top. Our cleaning process:

  1. Skipped metadata rows (rows 0–4 and a units row at index 6) when loading the file with pd.read_excel, and dropped the empty variables column.
  2. Combined date and time columns into single timestamps: OUTAGE.START.DATE + OUTAGE.START.TIME → OUTAGE.START (a pd.Timestamp), and likewise OUTAGE.RESTORATION.DATE + OUTAGE.RESTORATION.TIME → OUTAGE.RESTORATION. This was done using pd.to_datetime and pd.to_timedelta. We then dropped the four original date/time columns.
  3. The resulting cleaned DataFrame has 1,534 rows × 54 columns.

These cleaning steps reflect the data generating process: outage start and restoration are naturally single points in time, not separate date and time fields — the original split was an artifact of the spreadsheet format.

Step 3: Assessment of Missingness

NMAR Analysis

We believe the column DEMAND.LOSS.MW (missing in ~46% of rows) is likely NMAR. Our reasoning is that demand loss is only carefully measured and reported when the outage is significant enough to warrant detailed tracking. When the actual demand loss is very small or negligible (e.g., a brief localized outage), utilities may not bother recording the precise megawatt loss, so the value is left blank. In other words, the probability that DEMAND.LOSS.MW is missing depends on its own (unobserved) value — small demand losses are less likely to be recorded — which is the defining characteristic of NMAR.

Additional data that could make it MAR: If we obtained each utility's reporting threshold (the minimum MW loss that triggers mandatory reporting) and their internal data completeness policies, then the missingness could be explained by these external factors rather than the value itself, converting the missingness mechanism from NMAR to MAR.

Missingness Dependency

We analyzed the missingness of CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED (missing in ~29% of rows) with two permutation tests at the α = 0.05 significance level:

Test 1: Missingness vs. CAUSE.CATEGORY (TVD)

Result: Observed TVD ≈ 0.557, p-value ≈ 0.0 (out of 1,000 permutations, none produced a TVD as extreme). We reject the null hypothesis — the missingness of CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED does depend on the cause of the outage. For example, fuel supply emergencies and intentional attacks are far more likely to have missing customer counts than severe weather events. This means CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED is MAR (Missing At Random) with respect to CAUSE.CATEGORY.

Test 2: Missingness vs. TOTAL.PRICE (Difference in Means)

Result: Observed |diff in means| ≈ 0.118 cents/kWh, p-value ≈ 0.476. We fail to reject the null hypothesis — the average electricity price in a state does not appear to influence whether customer counts are recorded.

Step 4: Hypothesis Testing

Null (H₀): The mean OUTAGE.DURATION is the same for severe-weather outages and non-severe-weather outages. Any observed difference is due to random chance.

Alternative (H₁): Severe-weather outages have a longer mean duration than non-severe-weather outages (one-sided).

Test statistic: Difference in group means (mean duration of severe weather − mean duration of non-severe-weather).
Significance level: α = 0.05.

Why these choices: Our central question asks what drives outage duration, and severe weather is the most common cause category. A one-sided test is appropriate because we have a directional hypothesis motivated by the EDA. The difference in means is a natural and interpretable test statistic for comparing the central tendency of two groups.

Result: The observed difference was ~2,538 minutes with a p-value ≈ 0.0 (out of 10,000 permutations, none produced a difference as large). We reject the null hypothesis at α = 0.05.

The data provide strong evidence that severe-weather outages tend to have longer durations than non-severe-weather outages. This aligns with our EDA findings and supports including CAUSE.CATEGORY as a key feature in our prediction model. However, we note that this is an observational finding — we cannot claim a causal relationship from this test alone.

Step 5: Framing a Prediction Problem

Prediction problem: Predict OUTAGE.DURATION (outage duration in minutes).

Type: Regression — OUTAGE.DURATION is a continuous numeric variable ranging from 0 to ~108,000 minutes.

Response variable: OUTAGE.DURATION. We chose this because outage duration is the most direct measure of outage severity from a planning perspective, and our central question asks what factors most influence it.

Evaluation metric: Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE). We chose RMSE over alternatives because:

  • Unlike R², RMSE is on the same scale as the response variable (minutes), making it directly interpretable.
  • Unlike Mean Absolute Error (MAE), RMSE penalizes large prediction errors more heavily, which is appropriate here since severely under-predicting a long outage is costlier than slightly mis-predicting a short one.

Features available at time of prediction: At the moment an outage begins, we would plausibly know the cause category, climate region, month, climate anomaly level, state population, and urbanization percentage. We would not know CUSTOMERS.AFFECTED, DEMAND.LOSS.MW, or OUTAGE.RESTORATION at prediction time, since those are only known after or during the outage.

Step 7: Final Model

New Features Added

FeatureSourceTransformRationale
CLIMATE.REGION Nominal (9 regions) OneHotEncoder Different climate regions face different weather patterns and infrastructure quality; our pivot table showed that mean duration varies substantially across regions.
ANOMALY.LEVEL Quantitative QuantileTransformer Captures oceanic-atmospheric conditions (El Niño/La Niña) that influence storm severity. The quantile transform maps this to a uniform distribution, helping the tree-based model split more effectively on extreme values.
POPULATION Quantitative QuantileTransformer States with larger populations may have more complex grids but also more repair crews. Raw population is very right-skewed; the quantile transform normalizes the distribution.
POPPCT_URBAN Quantitative (0–100%) Passthrough Higher urbanization correlates with denser infrastructure and faster repair access. Already on a reasonable scale as a percentage.

Modeling Algorithm

We use a Random Forest Regressor because it naturally captures non-linear relationships and feature interactions (e.g., severe weather × certain climate regions may compound duration), and it is robust to the extreme outliers present in our heavy-tailed target variable.

Hyperparameter Tuning

We used GridSearchCV with 5-fold cross-validation, scoring by negative RMSE, over the following grid:

The scatter plot below shows predictions clustering closer to the diagonal (perfect prediction) line, though the model still struggles with extreme-duration outliers — a fundamental challenge given the heavy-tailed distribution.

Why the new features help (from a data-generating perspective):

  • Climate region captures regional infrastructure differences (age of grid, terrain, crew availability) and regional weather patterns that influence repair time.
  • Anomaly level reflects oceanic-atmospheric conditions that drive more intense storms, potentially prolonging outages.
  • Population proxies for grid complexity and available repair resources.
  • Urban percentage relates to infrastructure density; urban areas typically have faster restoration due to proximity of repair crews and equipment.
